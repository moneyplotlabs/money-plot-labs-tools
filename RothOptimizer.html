Starting Balances

Roth Basis = total lifetime Roth contributions (not growth). Can be withdrawn at any age, tax- and penalty-free. Must be ≤ Roth Balance.
Taxable Basis = what you originally paid for the holdings in your taxable account (cost basis). The balance above this is unrealized gain, taxed when withdrawn. Leave at $0 to assume the whole balance is gains (worst case). Must be ≤ Taxable Balance.

Retirement Trigger: Each of the 500 stochastic runs retires independently the first year total NW ≥ the target (Tool 3 method). The "Median Retire Age" card shows the P50 outcome; the P10–P90 range reflects sequence-of-returns uncertainty. Once retired, pre-tax funds are distributed via IRS 72(t) fixed amortization — equal annual withdrawals lasting the full planning horizon, no early-withdrawal penalty. Remaining spending draws from taxable first, then Roth basis.

Withdrawal order each year: (1) Pre-tax 72(t) — fixed amortized draw for full retirement, taken first. If this covers all spending, nothing else is touched. (2) Roth basis — spread flat to deplete exactly at 59½ (penalty-free at any age). (3) Taxable — amortized smoothly over full retirement to spread capital-gain realisations; gains and return-of-basis portions tracked separately. (4) Roth gains — last resort; free after 59½, 10% penalty before. After 59½ the order is: pre-tax 72(t) → taxable → full Roth (basis then gains, no penalty).

Withdrawal order is a simplifying assumption. This fixed sequence is not a recommendation. Real-world sourcing depends on factors this tool does not model — ACA premium-subsidy thresholds (taxable gains raise MAGI, while Roth basis and qualified Roth distributions do not), FAFSA income reporting for households with college-age children, current vs. expected future tax brackets, NIIT and IRMAA, state taxes, and capital-gain harvesting opportunities. The right order is individual and may change year to year. A visible consequence of this assumption: because the Roth-basis bridge is scheduled to deplete at 59½, the taxable draw is small before 59½ (it only fills the gap left after the bridge) and then steps up sharply once the bridge ends and taxable becomes the primary gap-filler. That jump is the assumption showing through, not a forced real-world outcome.

Key Question: Is your Roth basis enough to bridge retirement → 59½? If 72(t) + smooth taxable draw fall short before 59½, Roth basis fills the gap. If basis runs out too, Roth gains are tapped with a 10% penalty. The "Median Early Penalty" card quantifies this cost. Shifting more contributions to Roth grows your basis but shrinks the 72(t), which can increase reliance on Roth gains later — the slider finds the balance.

Contribution Limits (2025 · Household of 2): Employee 401k (pre-tax + Roth): $47,000 · Roth IRA: $14,000 · Mega-backdoor (after-tax → Roth): up to ~$93k minus employer match · Taxable: unlimited. No tax drag modeled — all accounts use the same gross return.

⚠️ Taxes not modeled. This tool ignores income taxes entirely — all withdrawals are treated as if tax-free. In reality, pre-tax 72(t) distributions are ordinary income, and the gains portion of taxable withdrawals are subject to capital gains tax. If your combined 72(t) + taxable-gains withdrawals push your retirement income into a higher bracket than your current marginal rate, you would have been better off contributing to Roth while working (pay tax now at the lower rate) rather than pre-tax (pay tax later at the higher rate). The opposite is true if your retirement withdrawals land in a lower bracket than today — pre-tax wins. The "right" split depends on your current vs. expected retirement tax rates, which this tool intentionally leaves for a future version.
