Employer/HSA deposits that repeat annually — e.g. $800 on Jan 1, $300 in mid-February.

What this does: HSA contributions usually land in a cash account earning little or nothing. Moving them into investments earning a real return is worth doing, but each transfer costs a flat fee. Transferring too often wastes money on fees; transferring too rarely leaves cash sitting idle. This tool finds the number of transfers and the exact days that maximize your final balance.

Method: contributions accumulate as a daily drip plus your recurring annual lump sums (all at 0% while uninvested). For any schedule of transfer days, each transferred block pays the fee and then compounds at your expected return to the end of the term; a final transfer closes out the remaining cash. The optimal schedule is found exactly (at day resolution) with a dynamic program over transfer days, and the chart of value vs. number of transfers shows where the benefit of compounding is overtaken by the cost of fees.

A common case: if your employer's HSA is with a provider like HealthEquity, contributing by payroll deduction saves FICA payroll tax, so there's good reason to keep contributing there. But the provider's in-house investments may carry a percentage fee and a limited menu, so some people periodically transfer money out to a broader brokerage — and it's that partial transfer-out that incurs a flat fee. This tool helps you decide how often paying it is worth it.

Note: this is an illustrative model, not tax or investment advice. It assumes a constant expected return and ignores taxes, the timing of market moves, and any HSA-specific investment thresholds or rules. Treat the schedule as guidance on cadence, not exact dates.
